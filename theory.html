1. Introduction to Open-Channel Hydraulics

Open-channel flow refers to the flow of liquids in channels where the surface is exposed to the atmosphere. This includes:

Natural Channels

Rivers, streams, creeks

Artificial Channels

Canals, drainage ditches

Urban Systems

Storm sewers, culverts

Industrial

Wastewater treatment

Key Concepts

Steady vs Unsteady Flow

Steady flow: Discharge constant with time
Unsteady flow: Discharge varies with time

Uniform vs Varied Flow

Uniform flow: Depth constant along channel
Varied flow: Depth changes along channel

Laminar vs Turbulent

Laminar: Smooth, layered flow (Re < 500)
Turbulent: Chaotic, mixed flow (Re > 2000)

Wetted Perimeter

$$P = b + 2y$$

Where:

  • \(P\) = Wetted perimeter (m)
  • \(b\) = Channel width (m)
  • \(y\) = Flow depth (m)

Hydraulic Radius

$$R = \frac{A}{P}$$

Where:

  • \(R\) = Hydraulic radius (m)
  • \(A\) = Flow area (m¬≤)
  • \(P\) = Wetted perimeter (m)

Characteristic length for flow resistance

Flow Velocity

$$V = \frac{Q}{A}$$

Where:

  • \(V\) = Mean velocity (m/s)
  • \(Q\) = Discharge (m¬≥/s)
  • \(A\) = Flow area (m¬≤)

3. Manning's Equation

$$Q = \frac{1}{n} A R^{2/3} S^{1/2}$$

The Manning equation is the most commonly used formula for uniform flow in open channels.

Symbol Variable Units Description
\(Q\) Discharge m¬≥/s Volumetric flow rate
\(n\) Manning's n - Roughness coefficient
\(A\) Area m¬≤ Cross-sectional area
\(R\) Hydraulic radius m A/P
\(S\) Slope m/m Energy gradient

Manning's n Values

Smooth Surfaces

  • Glass, PVC: 0.009 - 0.011
  • Concrete (finished): 0.012 - 0.014
  • Wood (planed): 0.010 - 0.015

Normal Surfaces

  • Earth (straight): 0.020 - 0.025
  • Gravel: 0.025 - 0.030
  • Rock cut: 0.030 - 0.035

Rough Surfaces

  • Earth (winding): 0.025 - 0.035
  • Natural streams: 0.030 - 0.050
  • Heavy vegetation: 0.050 - 0.150

4. Critical Flow & Froude Number

Critical Depth (Rectangular)

$$y_c = \sqrt[3]{\frac{Q^2}{g b^2}}$$

Froude Number

$$Fr = \frac{V}{\sqrt{g y}}$$

Flow Classification by Froude Number

Subcritical Flow

Fr < 1

Characteristics:

  • Tranquil, slow flow
  • Deep water surface
  • Downstream control
  • Disturbances travel upstream

Typical in: Rivers, mild slopes

Critical Flow

Fr = 1

Characteristics:

  • Minimum specific energy
  • Unstable transition
  • Maximum discharge for given energy
  • Occurs at control sections

Typical in: Weirs, crests, transitions

Supercritical Flow

Fr > 1

Characteristics:

  • Rapid, shooting flow
  • Shallow water surface
  • Upstream control
  • Disturbances travel downstream

Typical in: Steep chutes, spillways

$$\Delta E = \frac{(y_2 - y_1)^3}{4 y_1 y_2}$$

$$L_j \approx 6(y_2 - y_1)$$

Applications of Hydraulic Jump

Energy Dissipation

Reduces flow energy to prevent erosion

Mixing

Enhances mixing of chemicals or air

Flow Control

Maintains subcritical flow downstream

Aeration

Increases dissolved oxygen in water

6. Specific Energy Concept

$$E = y + \frac{V^2}{2g} = y + \frac{Q^2}{2g A^2}$$

Specific Energy (E) is the energy per unit weight of water measured relative to the channel bottom.

Pressure Head (y)

Depth of flow = static pressure energy

Velocity Head (V¬≤/2g)

Kinetic energy due to flow velocity

Specific Energy Diagram

The specific energy diagram shows the relationship between flow depth and energy:

Critical Point

Minimum energy for given discharge

Occurs at critical depth (y = y_c)

Subcritical Branch

Upper limb of the curve

Deep, slow flow (Fr < 1)

Supercritical Branch

Lower limb of the curve

Shallow, fast flow (Fr > 1)

Key Insight: For a given specific energy (except minimum), there are two possible depths: one subcritical and one supercritical. These are called alternate depths.
